Resistors

     The typical resistor is a tiny electronic device used to reduce the flow of current and instead increase voltage. Their construction consists of a very thin wire coil (covered in chemical paste) wrapped up inside of a carbon and metal shell, which is used to reduce the flow of electrons due to a combination of factors. The first being the fact that the thin wire prevents a large amount of electrons from flowing from one end to the other, the second being that the shape of the wire (a coil) contributes to electromagnetic resistance, discussed further in the ‘inductors’ section of the webpage, and the third being the chemical paste that surrounds the coiled wire, adding even more resistance to the equation. Since resistors are used to resist current in an electronic circuit, they are useful in applications where a larger source of electricity needs to be conformed to a component needing a smaller amount, and different ratings of resistors can be used to achieve this. Since resistors have so many applications, there is no ‘standard’ resistance, but is instead measured in a unit referred to as ohms. The higher the rating in ohms, the higher the resistance.
     Although resistors are commonly thought of as small electrical components that consist of a semi-cylindrical form, they are more prevalent than just this in the field of electronics. To be honest, any electrical component acts as a resistor in some way or another, even if its sole purpose is not directly to resist current. Examples of this include LEDs, motors, and even wires! Due to the fact that no device is perfect (not even superconductors), every electrical device either intentionally or unintentionally limits current since they are not able to efficiently output the same current that they have received.

Transistors

     The working of a transistor all comes down to its subatomic counterparts- a P-type section, which contains an excess of “positive” holes, and an N-type section, which contains an excess of “negative” electrons. Although they may seem similar, the P-type section is a mixture of silicon atoms and boron, aluminum, indium, and gallium, while the N-type section is a mixture of silicon atoms and phosphorus, along with arsenic antimony.
     In a configuration referred to as “NPN”, a thin layer of p-type is sandwiched between two larger layers of n-type, creating two “meeting points” for the n and p types, known as depletion regions. Since depletion regions are where an n-type meets a p-type, the excess electrons in the n-type oppose the excess holes in the p-type, so much so that they repel each other. Due to this repelling force, it is often hard for electrons to jump across the barrier, although if the circuit is wired in a certain way, this can be accomplished.
     If the current is biased where the negative terminal of a battery is connected to the n-type substance (along with the positive terminal of the battery being connected to the p-type substance), then current will flow well through this section of the transistor, and is known as “forward biasing”. This occurs because when the electrons (from the negative terminal of the battery) reach the n-type substrate, they give a forceful push (voltage) to the electrons that allow them to jump over to the other side of the depletion region, and to the positive terminal of the battery. However, if the negative terminal of the battery is connected to the p-type substance along with the positive terminal of the battery being connected to the n-type substance, then the electrons from the negative terminal of the battery will attract the holes in the p-type substrate along with the positive terminal of the battery attracting the n-type substrate, causing the depletion region to grow since the substance is clustered more towards the ends of the component. Every once in a while, very few electrons manage to venture across this almost impenetrable barrier, and this is referred to as leakage current.
     Now, what if one of the n/p-type junctions on the transistor was reverse-biased while the other was forward biased? Well, if this was done, then you would have turned your transistor into a working amplifier! Let’s look at how this works…

     If we wire the negative side of a battery to the n-type substance along with the positive side of a battery to the p-type substance, then a forward bias will be present and the electrons will flow in a loop. This is referred to as the “emitter/base” junction. If we also wire the negative side of a second battery to the first n-type section along with the positive side of a second battery to the second n-type section, a reverse bias will be produced, with the electrons from the negative side of the battery giving an “extra push” to the ones stuck in the forward bias’ loop. With this in place, the electrons just mentioned will have enough energy to overcome both depletion regions and will also be attracted to the second battery’s positive terminal, presented in the forward-biased circuit. This junction is referred to as the “emitter/collector” junction. Now, with this circuit complete, the “collector current” will present more current than what was fed into the circuit, to begin with. Due to this, an amplifier has just been created!
     It is amazing that you just learned how to create an amplifier out of a BJT transistor, but do you want to see the effect that this amplification of current has on a circuit? Well, since we know that resistors “resist” current but increase voltage when an increase in current is present (due to the collector current), the voltage will also increase, and therefore make the amplification more visible. To see an example of this, wire a motor directly to the battery and observe the result. Not much, right? Now wire the motor to the output of the collector circuit. Notice how it spins more vigorously.

Capacitors

     Capacitors are an electrical component that functions a bit like a battery. They are used in TV’s, cameras, and many other electronic devices. They store electric energy in order to release it at once, with a camera flash being a good example. Electricity is added to a capacitor in a process called charging, for several seconds. Then, the accumulated energy is quickly released through the xenon bulb. Capacitors consist of two conductors, called plates, and an insulator in between them, called the dielectric. The two plates inside a capacitor are wired up to two electrical connections on the outside of the capacitor, called terminals, which are like thin metal legs you can attach onto an electric circuit.
     One charges a capacitor by attaching it into an electric circuit. When the circuit is active, an electric charge gradually builds up on the plates. One plate gains a positive charge and the other plate gains an equal and opposite negative charge.If the circuit is deactivated, the capacitor keeps its electric charge. However, the interesting part is when you connect the capacitor to another circuit containing a motor, bulb, or other object of the sort, electricity will flow from the capacitor through the motor or lamp, depleting the capacitor’s stored energy until there's none remaining on the plates.
     Although capacitors have a very simple purpose of storing electric energy, this basic nature allows them to be used in a wide variety of situations and applications. Because the charging times can be precisely determined, they can be used as timers. They can be used as filters, restricting the signals that flow through a circuit. They can keep the voltage stable, since they can absorb excess power and release it when power is insufficient. They can even be used for tuning in objects like radios and TV’s!
     The amount of electrical energy a capacitor can store is its capacitance. The capacitance of a capacitor is comparable to a bucket: the bigger the bucket, the more water it can store; the bigger the capacitance, the more electricity a capacitor can store. To increase capacitance, there are three different methods. Firstly, you can increase the size of the plates. This simply makes the capacitor bigger. It’s the same efficiency, just a more powerful unit overall. The second way is to move the plates closer together. Finally, you can increase the efficiency of the dielectric, making it better at stopping the flow of electricity. Capacitors use dielectrics made from many different materials. In transistor radios, the tuning is carried out by a large variable capacitor that has nothing but air between its plates. In most electronic circuits, the capacitors are sealed components with dielectrics made of ceramics such as mica and glass, paper soaked in oil, or plastics such as mylar.
     Capacitance is measured in Farads, named for English scientist Michael Faraday. One farad is an extremely large unit of capacitance, so every day capacitors are measured in millionths of a Farad, or microfarads (μF), thousand-millionths of a Farad, or nanofarads (nF), and million-millionths of a Farad, or picofarads (pF). However, the largest capacitor in the world is 10,000 Farads!

Electromagnetism

     Since electrons produce an electric field, or charged field, (resulting in their negative charge) when a current is passed through a conductor, a small electromagnetic field is produced altogether due to these smaller subatomic fields. This means that the conductor acts as a magnet (due to the small electromagnetic field that it produces), and therefore has a north and a south pole to match. This, in turn, means that the electromagnet can interact with other fellow magnets and/or electromagnets, and produce the common effects of attraction (when opposite charges are present, such as + to -) or repulsion (when similar charges are present, such as + to +, or - to -). Since the current through a conductor is the underlying principle that produces the electromagnetic field, if the current is taken away, the electromagnetic field does so, also. Similarly, if the current was switched (such as the battery -supplying the conductor with the current- being switched), the electromagnetic field’s poles would also reverse, and what used to be the ‘north pole’ is now the ‘south pole’ of the magnet. The only problem with this method is that normal conductors conduct in a straight line (as shown in the first image), and therefore the electric fields of the individual electrons do not produce an electromagnetic field strong enough to have any effect on the other components around it. To concentrate the electric fields closer together, coils (or a conductor wrapped around into a loop) are used, and this creates a much stronger electromagnetic field, useful for interacting with objects around it. To change the strength of electromagnetic fields even further, different types of cores (cylindrical objects inserted between the coils) can be used. The most common types of cores include: air cores (no core), iron cores, and laminated cores (cores that are composed of many layers of a material, so that eddy current losses -a type of electromagnetic resistance- are lessened).

Inductors

     Inductors are electronic components that use the concept of electromagnetism (described in the section above) to perform complex tasks, such as inductive reactance (used to resist AC), or simply to slow down the change in voltage (when used in DC circuits). For example, if an inductor was wired up in a circuit consisting of a power supply, resistor (if needed), and LED, the LED would slowly light up (depending on the amount of times the wire has been coiled, the thickness of the wire, and if a core was inserted inside of the coils) due to its electromagnetic properties. This occurs, because as the electrons flow through one side of the coil, the strong electromagnetic field produced on each one of the rings resists the electrons in the rings nearest to it, creating a very high resistance to begin with. As time progresses, current starts to flow more steadily through the coil, and electromagnetic resistance decreases. This creates a more stable environment. Right before the voltage source is disconnected, however, the inductor tends to build up a resistance once again, due to the fact that the electromagnetic field is collapsing. This results in an uneven power dissipation to the rest of the circuit. To recap, inductors can be described as the opposite of a capacitor due to the fact that they build up charge slowly at first, and then release it slowly in the opposite direction, while capacitors build up charge in a relatively fast manner and release it in a similar way following the same direction that it was charged.      Although not as widely used as capacitors, inductors are used in various applications, also. One of the most common applications for inductors is relays (electromagnetic switches), which makes use of inductors for the sole purpose of switching on and off parts of a circuit using a physical infrastructure. Beyond this, however, inductors are also used in what are usually referred to as ‘transistor radios’, or small radios used for personal use. Since alternating current must be generated inside of a transistor radio to properly output sound waves, an inverter (a device that transforms DC to AC) must be included so that two common AA batteries can be used to power the device. In order to create an inverter, a combination of an inductor and a capacitor must be used in a specific pattern to generate this oscillating waveform (although this is an advanced concept, so it will not fully be discussed in this tutorial).

DC Motors

     Now, with this concept of electromagnetism and coils in mind, take a look at the diagram below. This is a diagram representing a very simple DC motor. As you might notice, the annotations reveal 5 major types of devices- two brushes, one split ring (also known as a commutator), one DC power supply, two large north and south pole magnets, and an armature (the name of the looped device connected to the commutator). When a current (generated by the DC battery) is passed to the brushes (which make contact with the commutator), a charge is presented to each of the split rings on the commutator –since they are split, they keep their charges separate, so whichever side of the battery each separate split ring is touching, it will take on that charge of the battery (for example, the split ring on the negative side of the battery will take a negative charge and the split ring on the positive side of the battery will take a positive charge). With this concept in mind, think of the armature as a coil. Since the split rings are connected separately to the armature, when current is passed from each of the split rings (and since current moves from the negative side to the positive side), a current will be instilled in the armature's coil, resulting in a generated electromagnet. Take a minute to recap and think about this, since this is a pretty complicated concept.

     Alright, now that you have had a little bit of time to think about it, notice the permanent magnets (represented by the north and south poles) surrounding the armature electromagnet. Also, notice the charges of the battery. Due to this, the split ring on the left side will always take on a negative charge and the split ring on the right side will always take on a positive charge (in this diagram). Therefore, since this creates a negative charge in the electromagnet on the left side, and there is also a permanent magnet with its north pole on the left side, these two magnets will repel each other. Since the split ring and the armature is connected to a shaft called the rotor of a motor, the rotor will spin due to this repulsion of the stator (magnets that surround the armature and are permanent magnets in this scenario). This is why a motor spins. Due to the design of the split rings in the commutator, when the armature makes a 180-degree turn, because of the repulsion generated by the two magnetic poles, the charges of the split rings will reverse, because what was previously the left split ring has now rotated into the right split ring’s spot, and vice versa. Now, when the battery supplies current from its negative pole to the left side like before, the current left split ring (former right split ring) becomes negative, resulting in repulsion between the left side of the armature electromagnet and the north pole of the permanent magnet once again. Therefore, this results in continuous motion of the rotor, until the battery stops supplying current or is removed from the equation.
     To see what a real DC motor looks like, please look below, and notice how the armature looks a lot different along with the stator being composed of electromagnets instead of permanent magnets. Do not be scared by this. This works the same way as the diagram I just described to you, it just has more electromagnets on its armature and is arranged slightly differently so that the rotor can rotate faster (measured in RPM, or Rotations Per Minute). Also, the replacement of the permanent magnets with electromagnets makes no difference, except that this can also improve efficiency.
